Flooring Acoustic Ratings: IIC and STC Explained


By the Editorial Team | Published: July 2026 | Last Updated: July 2026

The Ratings That Decide What the Downstairs Neighbor Hears

Flooring's acoustic ratings live in obscurity until someone moves into a condo - then IIC and STC become the two most important numbers on the spec sheet. They measure how much sound a floor-ceiling assembly stops, they appear in building codes and homeowners-association rules with hard minimums, and they are chronically misread, because the number on a product brochure and the performance in an actual building are related but far from identical. Anyone choosing flooring for an upper floor, a condo, or a rental stack benefits from ten minutes with the acoustics.

Two Ratings for Two Kinds of Sound

Sound reaches the room below by two routes, and each has its own metric. Impact noise is physical: footsteps, dropped toys, chair scrapes - energy hammered directly into the floor structure. The Impact Insulation Class (IIC) rates how well the assembly blocks it, measured by running a standardized tapping machine on the floor above and recording what arrives below. Airborne noise is vibrational through air: voices, televisions, music - sound waves striking the assembly and re-radiating beneath. The Sound Transmission Class (STC) rates that path. Both scales read the same direction - higher blocks more - and building codes commonly set 50 as the minimum for each in multifamily construction, with premium buildings and HOAs demanding more.

The Fine Print: Assemblies, Not Products

Here is the misreading that causes condo disputes: IIC and STC describe the entire floor-ceiling sandwich - flooring, underlayment, subfloor, joists or slab, insulation, and the ceiling below - tested together in a laboratory. A vinyl plank advertising "IIC 71" earned that number on one specific assembly, usually a concrete slab with a suspended ceiling; the same plank over bare wood framing delivers dramatically less. Comparing products means comparing them on the same stated assembly, and satisfying an HOA means testing documentation for an assembly resembling the actual building. The honest per-product metric is the Delta IIC - how many points the flooring and pad add over the bare structure - which manufacturers increasingly publish and which travels between buildings far better than the headline number.

Where the Improvement Actually Comes From

Impact performance is bought at the resilient layers - the springy interruptions that keep footstep energy out of the structure. Soft surfaces are the champions (carpet and pad post enormous IIC numbers effortlessly), which is why hard-floor renovations are what trigger acoustic rules. For hard surfaces, the work is done by the underlayment or attached pad: cork, foam, rubber, and fiber layers each with rated contributions, chosen to match the flooring type and the HOA's target. Airborne performance barely notices the floor covering; STC is won with mass (concrete, double drywall), insulation in the cavity, and decoupling (resilient channels, isolation clips) - assembly-level construction, not plank selection. The practical division of labor: pick flooring and pad for IIC, and if STC is short, the fix lives in the ceiling below.

Reading a Spec Sheet Without Being Fooled

Three habits protect a buyer. Check the assembly behind any big number - "tested over 6-inch concrete with ceiling" is a different world from a wood-framed triple-decker. Prefer Delta ratings for comparing products head to head. And treat lab numbers as ceilings: field performance (measured as FIIC/FSTC in the actual building) typically lands several points below the lab, which is why experienced boards write minimums with margin. The pad-and-plank combination that satisfies all of this is a solvable, product-level problem - part of the same performance-tier reading that separates resilient products generally.

Frequently Asked Questions

What IIC rating do I need for a condo?

The building code baseline is commonly IIC 50, but the binding number is the HOA's - 55 to 60+ is routine in newer or upscale buildings, and some specify the exact underlayment. Get the requirement in writing before buying flooring, and keep the test documentation for approval.

Is a higher IIC number always a quieter floor?

Between identical assemblies, yes. Between brochures, not necessarily - the number belongs to the tested sandwich, and a lighter assembly with an honest number can outperform a heavy-assembly headline. Compare on matching assemblies or use Delta values.

Do rugs really change anything?

Dramatically - a rug and pad over a hard floor adds impact isolation that rivals engineered solutions, which is why "rug rules" (covering a percentage of floors) survive in older condo documents. They do nothing for airborne sound.

Why can I still hear bass and voices with a high-IIC floor?

Because those are airborne (STC) events, and the floor covering barely touches them. Persistent voice and TV transmission points at the assembly - insulation, mass, decoupling - usually addressed from the ceiling side below.

Can underlayment be doubled for more quiet?

Not under click-together floors - excess softness lets locking joints flex and fail, and most warranties prohibit stacked pads. Choose a single rated pad appropriate to the product, or move to flooring with a better attached layer.

What's the quietest hard-surface option?

Resilient products with quality attached pads over a rated underlayment post the strongest hard-surface impact numbers - cork underlayers and rubber layers leading. Nothing hard matches carpet; the game is closing the gap while keeping the floor you want.

Who verifies acoustic performance after installation?

Acoustic consultants run field tests (FIIC/FSTC) in occupied buildings when disputes or approvals demand proof. Expect field results a few points under lab ratings - normal, and the reason to buy margin above the minimum rather than at it.

Do acoustic requirements apply to single-family homes?

Legally, rarely - impact codes target multifamily stacks. Practically, the same physics runs every two-story house: hard-floored kids' rooms over a primary suite, or a home office under a hallway, benefit from exactly the same rated pads and rug strategies condos are forced into. Treating an upstairs reflooring as a small acoustic project costs a few cents per square foot at install and is nearly impossible to retrofit later.
